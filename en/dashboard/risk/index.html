Your age group has an above average risk of identity theft
Identity theft is a real problem and, unfortunately for people your age, you have a greater than average risk. A popular method for targeting people in your age group is recorded phone calls with messages related to the Canada Revenue Agency, your Social Insurance Number, and immigration scams. If you receive a threatening call demanding payment related to fines or unpaid taxes, hang up — look up the number yourself and call back to confirm.
Married individuals have an above average risk of identity theft
Identity theft is something that married people especially need to be aware of. Your spouse knows a lot of sensitive information about you and could accidentally disclose it to scammers — putting you both at risk. Online monitoring of your personal information and financial accounts can help protect you from becoming a victim.
Your income indicates you have a high risk of identity theft
Identity theft is more likely to affect individuals in higher-income households. The more money you make, the more likely you are to become a target for identity thieves. A product like ID Assist that offers both online monitoring and credit bureau monitoring can alert you to threats so you can take action as quickly as possible.
Your education level indicates a high risk of identity theft

Enrolling in a product like ID Assist that monitors both your personal information and your credit bureau files can alert you to threats so you can act as quickly as possible.
Not having children puts you at a lower risk of identity theft
Children under 18 don't have credit reports, which can make them attractive targets for criminals who open accounts in their name — fraud that often goes undetected for years. Since this doesn't apply to your household, this factor works in your favour.

You use a password manager — a great habit
Using a password manager means you're far more likely to have unique, complex passwords across all of your accounts. This is one of the single most effective protections against credential-based attacks. Keep it up!
Your password habits put you at above average risk for some accounts
Adding special characters to passwords is a good start — but using a limited number of password combinations puts you at above average risk for those accounts. You should always use complex passwords (letters, numbers, and special characters) or passphrases: four random words that make sense to you but are hard for criminals to guess. Add numbers and symbols to the passphrase and you'll be even more secure. Use a different password for every site.
You change your passwords regularly
Regularly rotating your passwords — especially after any breach notification — significantly reduces the window of opportunity for criminals. This is a strong habit that contributes to your below-average risk in this section.

Understanding Your Score
How the Risk Assessment works
1

What the score measures

Your Risk Assessment reflects your likelihood of becoming a victim of identity theft based on your personal habits, behaviours, and profile — not whether you've already been affected. A lower score means fewer risk factors in your profile.

2

How your score is calculated

Scores are based on your responses across four categories: Demographics, Password Habits, Social Media Use, and Online Security Practices. Each category is weighted against Canadian identity theft patterns.

3

Keeping your score current

Your risk profile changes as your habits and circumstances evolve. We recommend retaking the assessment every 6 months, or whenever you experience a significant life change — such as a new job, home, or social media account.
